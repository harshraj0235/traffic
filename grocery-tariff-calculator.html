How Tariffs Are Raising Your Grocery Bill in

The US imports about 15% of its food supply, including much of its fresh produce, seafood, specialty items, coffee, and beverages. Every one of these imports now faces higher tariffs.

Most Affected Grocery Items

  • Avocados (Mexico, 25% tariff): The US gets 80% of avocados from Mexico. The 25% IEEPA tariff has increased prices by $0.40-$0.75 per avocado.
  • Olive Oil (EU, 20% tariff): European olive oil faces a 20% tariff, adding $2-$4 per bottle.
  • Coffee (Brazil, 10% tariff): Brazilian and Colombian coffee faces the 10% baseline tariff, adding $1-$2 per pound.
  • Wine (EU, 20% tariff): European wines face a 20% tariff, adding $3-$5 per bottle.
  • Cheese (EU/Canada, 20-25%): Imported cheeses from France, Italy, and Switzerland face significant tariff increases.
  • Seafood (Various, 10-36%): Shrimp from Thailand (36%), salmon from Canada (25%), and various fish face increased tariffs.

📋 Key Grocery Tariff Facts

  • Mexico is the #1 source of US fresh produce imports
  • The average family pays $300-$600/year more in food tariffs
  • Domestic produce (US-grown) is tariff-free
  • Organic imports face the same tariff rates as conventional
  • Seasonal produce may see higher tariff impact during off-season
  • Farmers markets and local producers offer tariff-free alternatives

Grocery Tariff FAQ

Common questions about tariffs on food and groceries.

Are tariffs raising grocery prices?+

Yes. About 15% of US food is imported, and all imported food now faces tariffs of 10-25%+. Fresh produce from Mexico (avocados, tomatoes, berries), European specialty items (olive oil, cheese, wine), and coffee/tea are most affected.

How much more am I paying for groceries?+

The average American family is estimated to pay $300-$600 more per year in food-related tariff costs. This varies based on diet — families that consume more imported produce, European foods, or seafood are paying more.

How can I avoid tariffs on food?+

Buy domestically-produced food whenever possible. Shop at farmers markets, buy seasonal produce, choose US-grown alternatives, and look for "Product of USA" labels. Domestic production is completely tariff-free.

Are organic food imports affected?+

Yes. Organic imports face the same tariff rates as conventional products. There is no exemption for organic certification. If it's imported, it faces the applicable tariff rate based on country of origin and product type.
