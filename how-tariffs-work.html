How Do Tariffs Work?
A Simple Guide for Consumers

Everything you need to know about US tariffs in plain English — who pays them, how they affect prices, and what changed in 2025-2026.

What Is a Tariff?

A tariff is a tax that a government charges on goods imported from another country. When a product crosses the US border, the company importing it must pay this tax to US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) before the product can be sold in America.

Think of it like a toll booth at the border — except instead of paying to cross a bridge, importers pay a percentage of the product's value to bring goods into the country.

📌 Key Facts About US Tariffs

  • Tariffs are paid by the importing company, not the foreign country or manufacturer
  • The cost is almost always passed on to consumers through higher prices
  • The average US tariff rate in 2026 is approximately 18-22% across all imports
  • Before 2025, the average effective tariff rate was only about 2-3%
  • Tariffs generated over $80 billion in government revenue in 2025

How Do Tariffs Affect Prices?

When an importer pays a tariff, they have three choices:

  1. Pass the full cost to consumers — This is most common. If a $100 product has a 30% tariff, the importer pays $30 in duties and raises the price to $130.
  2. Absorb some of the cost — The company accepts lower profit margins. This is less common and usually temporary.
  3. Find a cheaper supplier — The company may switch to a manufacturer in a country with lower tariffs.

Research from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York and multiple universities has consistently found that nearly 100% of tariff costs from the 2018-2025 trade actions were passed on to US consumers and businesses.

Types of US Tariffs in 2026

The current US tariff system involves several overlapping layers:

1. Section 122 Baseline Tariff (10%)

Announced on April 2, 2025 ("Liberation Day"), this imposes a minimum 10% tariff on ALL imports from ALL countries. This is the floor — no country pays less than 10%.

2. Reciprocal Tariffs (10-50%)

Higher tariff rates calculated based on what the US says other countries charge on American goods. For example, Vietnam faces 46%, Taiwan faces 32%, and India faces 26%.

3. Section 301 Tariffs (China-Specific)

Originally from the 2018-2019 trade war, these add 7.5-25% additional tariffs on specific Chinese product categories like electronics, clothing, and machinery.

4. IEEPA Tariffs (Fentanyl-Related)

Tariffs on China (20%), Mexico (25%), and Canada (25%) imposed under emergency powers citing fentanyl trafficking concerns.

5. Sector-Specific Tariffs

25% on all imported vehicles regardless of country, and 25% on steel and aluminum from all countries.

Who Really Pays Tariffs?

Despite common misconceptions, tariffs are not paid by foreign countries or foreign companies. Here's the actual chain of payment:

  1. The US importer (an American company) pays the tariff to US Customs
  2. The importer raises wholesale prices to offset the tariff cost
  3. Retailers pass the cost to shoppers through higher shelf prices
  4. You, the consumer, ultimately pay the tariff through higher prices on everyday goods

How to Reduce Your Tariff Burden

While you can't avoid tariffs entirely, there are strategies to minimize their impact:

  • Buy American-made products — Domestically manufactured goods don't carry import tariffs
  • Check country of origin labels — The same product may come from countries with different tariff rates
  • Consider refurbished or used goods — These avoid new import tariffs
  • Buy from countries with lower tariffs — UK (10%) vs. China (30-54%) makes a big difference
  • Time your purchases — Watch for trade deal announcements that may temporarily lower rates

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about how tariffs work

Do tariffs make goods more expensive?+

Yes. Extensive research from the Federal Reserve, academic economists, and nonpartisan think tanks has consistently found that tariff costs are passed through to consumer prices. The Tax Foundation estimates the current tariffs cost the average American household about $1,000-$1,745 per year in higher prices.

Does the foreign country pay the tariff?+

No. Tariffs are paid by US-based importing companies when goods cross the US border. The cost is then passed through the supply chain to American consumers in the form of higher retail prices. The foreign manufacturer or government does not pay the tariff.

What is the current tariff rate on Chinese goods?+

Chinese goods face a complex, layered tariff structure. The base rate is approximately 30% (from the May 2025 Geneva deal), but Section 301 duties add 7.5-25% on top for specific categories like electronics and clothing. Effective rates on Chinese goods range from 30% to over 54% depending on the product.

Are there any exemptions from tariffs?+

Some products have received temporary exemptions, particularly certain pharmaceuticals and critical minerals. USMCA-compliant goods from Canada and Mexico may qualify for reduced rates. However, most consumer goods are subject to full tariff rates. The $800 de minimis duty-free threshold has been suspended for Chinese shipments.

Will tariffs go down in the future?+

It depends on ongoing trade negotiations and political developments. Some tariffs may be reduced through bilateral trade deals (the US-UK deal is under negotiation). In February 2026, the Supreme Court struck down certain IEEPA tariffs, which could reduce some rates. However, Section 301 tariffs on China and the 10% global baseline are likely to remain for the foreseeable future.

How do tariffs affect inflation?+

Tariffs contribute to inflation by raising the cost of imported goods. The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis and multiple economic models estimate that 2025 tariffs added 0.5-1.5 percentage points to the US inflation rate. Categories most affected include electronics, clothing, vehicles, and fresh produce.
