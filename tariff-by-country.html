Understanding US Tariff Rates by Country ()

US tariff rates are determined by a combination of trade policies: the 10% global baseline tariff (Section 122), country-specific reciprocal tariffs, IEEPA tariffs, and product-specific duties (Section 301 for China). Here's a breakdown by region:

Highest Tariff Countries

  • China (30% base + Section 301): The highest rates at 37-54%+ effective, depending on product. See China tariff calculator.
  • Vietnam (46%): High reciprocal tariff despite being a manufacturing hub for Nike, Samsung and many US brands.
  • Bangladesh (37%): Major garment exporter — clothing prices from Bangladesh up 25-40%.
  • Thailand (36%): Electronics and auto parts significantly impacted.

USMCA Partners

  • Mexico (25%): IEEPA tariff, not reciprocal. USMCA-compliant goods may qualify for reduced rates. See Mexico/Canada details.
  • Canada (25%): Same IEEPA basis as Mexico. Lumber, oil, and auto industries most affected.

Allied Nations

  • EU (20%), Japan (24%), South Korea (25%): Close allies facing significant reciprocal tariffs, especially on vehicles.
  • UK (10%): Minimum baseline rate. US-UK trade deal could reduce this further.

Country Tariff FAQ

What is the minimum US tariff rate?+

The minimum tariff rate is 10% — the global baseline imposed under Section 122 on April 2, 2025. This applies to all imports from all countries, with many countries facing additional reciprocal tariffs on top.

Which country has the lowest tariff?+

The UK and Brazil face the lowest rates at the 10% global baseline. A US-UK trade deal could potentially reduce this further.

Why does China have the highest tariffs?+

China's high rates are the result of multiple overlapping tariff actions: pre-existing Section 301 tariffs from the 2018-2019 trade war, IEEPA tariffs citing fentanyl, and reciprocal tariffs. These compound to create effective rates of 37-54%+.

Do USMCA goods from Mexico/Canada avoid tariffs?+

USMCA-compliant goods may qualify for reduced or zero duty rates. However, the IEEPA tariffs (25%) override USMCA benefits in many cases. The situation depends on the specific product and its USMCA compliance status.
