US-EU Tariffs Explained (2026)

The European Union is a massive trading bloc, and its exports to the US face a 13% reciprocal tariff. However, the most significant impact is on the automotive sector, which is subject to a 25% import tariff.

📌 EU Tariff Key Facts

  • Reciprocal rate: 13% on most goods from EU member states
  • Auto tariff: 25% on imported vehicles (affects German and Italian automakers heavily)
  • Luxury goods: 13% on French and Italian fashion, leather goods, and cosmetics
  • Food & Beverage: 13% on European wine, cheese, olive oil, and spirits
  • Machinery: High impact on specialized industrial equipment from Germany

Most Affected European Imports

The categories most impacted by the US-EU tariff include:

  • Vehicles (Germany, Italy, Sweden): A Porsche, Audi, Mercedes, or BMW imported from Europe faces a 25% tariff, adding thousands to the sticker price. (Note: Many German brands have US factories that avoid this tariff).
  • Luxury Fashion (France, Italy): Brands like LVMH, Gucci, Prada, and Chanel face a 13% tariff, increasing the cost of high-end apparel and accessories.
  • Wine & Spirits (France, Italy, Spain): European wines, champagne, and certain spirits face the 13% reciprocal tariff.
  • Specialty Foods: Italian olive oil, French cheese, and Spanish meats cost more for US consumers.

💡 Exemption Note: UK is Separate

Since Brexit, the United Kingdom is no longer part of the EU. The US and UK are negotiating a separate bilateral trade agreement, and UK goods may face different rates. See our UK Import Duty Calculator for details.

EU Tariff FAQ

Why is the EU tariff 13%?+

The 13% tariff is a "reciprocal" tariff. The US administration calculated that the EU applies an average 13% tariff on American goods, so the US matches that rate for EU imports.

Does the 25% auto tariff apply to all European cars?+

It applies to all vehicles assembled in Europe and imported into the US. However, automakers like BMW (Spartanburg, SC), Mercedes-Benz (Vance, AL), and Volkswagen (Chattanooga, TN) have large US factories. Vehicles assembled in these US plants do not pay the import tariff.

Are Airbus planes subject to tariffs?+

Commercial aircraft have been subject to complex, fluctuating tariffs due to long-standing disputes at the WTO regarding aircraft subsidies (the Boeing-Airbus dispute), separate from the general 13% reciprocal rate.

Will the US and EU negotiate a new trade deal?+

Negotiations are possible, but historically difficult. Sticking points often include agricultural access (the EU strictly regulates GMOs and hormone-treated beef) and digital services taxes.
